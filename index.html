current reads

A Fine Balance
by Rohinton Mistry

An odyssey through late 20th century India, gazing at the rise of unchecked political corruption, religious persecution, poverty, and pollution from the perspective of troubled yet hopeful protagonists tackling problem after problem, often with open distrust of each other considering the circumstances. I'm about halfway through and the constantly degrading quality of life, the straw that broke the camel's back being seemingly a page a way is pretty draining; I cannot imagine living through such a profound loss of identity and helplessness to change a blatantly self-serving system.

Unknown Quantity
by Rohinton Mistry

Fascinating tour through the invention of algebra, with individual chapters for group theory, linear algebra, ring theory, and algebraic geometry, the last of which I haven't read yet. The primers are a great introduction to upper level math, by way of a few motivating examples for each field. I particularly enjoyed the section on roots of unity, although I'm itching to actually absorb the math; it's quite easy to lose the plot if you're not familiar with the rigorous definitions. Also, I have to mention even knowing very little about math history, you can tell from the prose itself that the author is biased, most notably, referring to more prolific mathematicians as smarter and better than less prolific ones, which he implies don't deserve to be as famous.

In the Palm of Your Hand
by Rohinton Mistry

Honestly, as someone who loves to write poetry, I struggle with the fact that I've read very little. It's another one of my countless interests that someday I'll find time to really dive headfirst into, but for now this book had been serving as a gentle introduction to the ethos behind poetry and why it communicates emotion so well. I love the chapter-specific prompts, isolating one aspect to focus on when writing for yourself. I did take this book from one of my college's common rooms, but once I'm finished I will definitely put it back; more students deserve to read this.
