permission

Saturday, 12 July 2025

I'm cringe. I constantly feel things that others have felt before me, that people are feeling in this current moment. My instinct is to express myself--I've written poems, fanfiction, journal entries, essays, letters--but it always comes with the undercurrent of illegitimacy, of impropriety, a tradesman's scam, a jester's trick revealed.

In a capitalist society, how dare I create what has already been created, more eloquently, more succinctly than I could? As a matter of fact, how dare I spend the time to create in the first place, in a dying medium, incapable of the virality that has become our measure of success?

When I run, when I climb, when I open my eyes in the morning and watch the sun trace a path on my bedroom wall, I'm repeating what thousands of people have done before me, and usually, I'm repeating myself. And yet, we all--aspirationally, maybe--find beauty, if not purpose, in the minutia of living. Most of us consciously search for retellings even, whether just for validation or to further digest common themes, to make slightly different decisions and hopefully, live slightly happier lives. Our individualistic societal values, the zero-sum game we've created, is incompatible with the collectivist desires that fuel real prosperity. I won't say they're innate, as many people I've known have contradicted that, but we evolved to embrace community; our strength, and happiness, comes from feeling more connected, from working together and sharing our lives.

I've tried to be a better person and felt selfish, and tried to better the world and felt empty. My standards were someone else's. I felt watched, judged in my own home, performative. Because creating anything wanting 'success' is performative. I'm tired of feeling guilty for doing what I love.

I love an extended metaphor, a thematic story, a resurfaced memory--more than anything, I love to feel. It might be the only thing that we still have in common. Sometimes, it's the only thing that feels real at all.

Welcome to a place you used to know well. A place you buried, and then romanticized, only acknowledged when strict conditions of secrecy, of anonymity, distance of hindsight or foresight further tint the glasses. Take a deep breath and wipe the dust off the counter; you're moving in for good this time.

meta glasses and respect

Monday, 29 July 2025

I've just recently had a surgery. It's my first one, so I was overwhelmed; not by the procedure itself, but by a recovery process that I had no precident for and would have to nonetheless manage with little professional oversight. I was trying--and succeeding--to think purely abstractly, reminising on books I was reading, jotting down ideas for poems or song lyrics, and doodling in the journal I keep in my bag.

My doctor walked in, and the illusion was shattered. "Can I ask you a stupid question?"
There are no stupid questions, etc, etc, until I finally spit it out---"Are you gonna wear those during the surgery?" He was wearing Meta's Rayban partnership glasses, the twin cameras on the frames glinting in the light.

My dad also has a pair, bright orange. I've had to tell him not to wear them around me. My excuse is privacy, which I think it quite fair considering Meta's long history of privacy violations, starting from changing our relationship to social media by introducing an endlessly refeshing feed, to blatantly anti-democratic political manipulation with Cambridge Analytica, to literal data harvesting. (If you're curious, I would reccomend this deep dive by Tantacrul.) Sure, the device's homepage implies that it is not recording when powered off (although the privacy policy does not explicitly state this), but some prelimary research reveals the inevitable creep toward data collection.

Since April 2025, the glasses now come with AI camera features enabled by default, meaning unless you disable the 'wake word' "Hey Meta", which is obstensibly an unrelated feature, your photos and videos are "sent from the Meta AI App to Meta servers to support additional photo and video features" which includes being used as training data. In order to ensure your videos are not processed or shared, you are forced to use the features of the glasses by physically interacting with the buttons, completely defeating the futuristic feeling emphasized by the marketing, which I assume is a main reason people buy these.

This may seem innocuous, as your personal identity may seem irrelevant to the training process, but even if the data is anonymized (which there is no mention of in the privacy policy), you are contributing a very valuable resource (data) to a company with a long history of disregarding human wellbeing and creating addictive technology that many agree pits us against each other and makes us more lonely, anxious, and depressed than ever. My problem with this product goes beyond privacy concerns. Although I definitely have them, it is somewhat debatable to which extent AI can recall the data it was trained on.
